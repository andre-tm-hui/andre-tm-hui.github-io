VR Drum Simulator

A virtual reality drumming simulator, designed to be as true-to-life as possible, with responsive interactions. This simulator includes:

  • Assortment of kit parts: Snare, Kick, Hi-Hat, 2 Crashes, 3 Toms and a Ride Cymbal, all modelled in Blender
  • Specific interactions including Rimshots on the snare, an alternate ride tone based on hit position and the ability to choke cymbals
  • Kit I/O: save and load different kit setups easily
  • Play your own music: play audio files in the game by placing audio files in a designated folder
  • MIDI I/O: connect the virtual kit to your DAW/plugin of choice (requires loopMIDI for Windows)
  • Analog kick and hi-hat pedal interactions, allowing control over their dynamics

Motive

This project was my first independent Unity project, away from university. Having more time to dedicate to game and VR development, I wanted to make something for VR that wasn't too ambitious for a programmer, yet lined up with my interests. Being a guitarist of 10 years, my mind immediately turned to music and instruments.

I chose drums specifically because it seemed to be the most achievable with modern consumer tech, as opposed to a piano or guitar that requires precise finger tracking.

Having worked on this project for half a year, if I were to take this project even further, I would like to expand the amount of instruments available in the game, with the goal of allowing players to collaborate remotely in a jam room, and play music together.

Challenges:

  • Collision causing multiple audio events: solved by setting a timeout equivalent to the world-record for fastest one-handed drum roll.
  • Missed collisions due to drumsticks passing through the entire collider in a single frame: solved by interpolating the drumstick's path and using raycasts to check for collisions.
  • Single drum strokes 'hitting through' multiple drums: only process the first raycast collision point found (see above).
  • No native support for MIDI output: learned C++ and used JUCE framework to develop a Unity Native Plugin that outputs to a Virtual MIDI Device.
  • Reducing unintentional strikes: find the normal to the top surface of a drum/cymbal, and compare the velocity of the stick to the normal. If the angle between the two vectors is less than a defined threshold, initiate an audio event.
  • No freely available, aesthetic drum 3D models: learned Blender and created custom drum assets for the game.
  • XR UI design: iterated through several different UI designs to determine the most intuitive and comfortable design.

Virtual Soundtool

A tool aimed at livestreamers, or anyone who wants to have more fun in their voicechats. Features:

  • Custom hotkeys: add, remove and change hotkeys
  • Realtime sampling: hold an empty hotkey to start recording audio from your microphone, system audio or both
  • Soundboard: press a hotkey to play the audio clip associated to the key
  • Voice changer: press a hotkey to toggle voice effects. Currently, reverb is available.

Motive

Having occasionally tuned in to livestreams since 2018, I learnt of the GoXLR - a USB mixer/sampler/soundboard/voice-changer. A quick google search and $500 pricetag later, I turned to my IDE and started seeing what I could do with Pyaudio and Python. What resulted was a functioning, but inelegant hotkey sampler and soundboard. By then, summer was over and I had to return to university, so the project was dropped for a while.

Fast forward to 2022, after reaching a comfortable point with my VR drum simulator, I chose to revisit this project, with the goal of turning it into something any person can use. Immediately, that rules out developing in Python, being unable to compile the code into a portable executable. Having got a taster for C++ through custom native Unity plugins, I wanted to get more familiar with the language, and knew that it was commonly used for writing distributable software. And so, I restarted the project.

Challenges:

  • New language: C++ is a lower-leveled relative to other languages like C#, Python, Java, so I had to familiarize myself with concepts such as pointers and addresses, in the context of C++.
  • Installing libraries: in Python, installing new libraries is as simple as calling "pip install x". C++ is more hands on, requiring you to either find pre-compiled libraries (often out of date), or compile libraries yourself in Visual Studio or using CMake, and then link the libraries to the project.
  • Virtual audio cables: the entire project relies on the ability to output audio to an input device, which is not natively supported. Luckily, VB-Audio makes a free virtual audio cable driver that allows just that.
  • Audio processing: I had to study digital audio, including file formats for I/O with Libsndfile, and raw audio data in PortAudio. To build on that, I had studied algorithms for implementing digital audio effects, such as reverberators and phase vocoders.
  • Windows hooks: in order to make functioning hotkeys, I learnt a bit about Windows hooks, allowing for background listening of keyboard inputs, meaning hotkeys can be activated regardless of which window is in focus.

VR Piano Simulator

A virtual reality piano/keyboard simulator, in very early development, which:

  • Maps a piano to a flat surface: place your hands on a flat surface for 3 seconds to spawn a piano on that surface. This solves the lack of tactile feedback as found in other implementations.
  • Uses hand-tracking: currently using Oculus hand-tracking, but I might swap to LucasVRTech's VR Gloves to improve accuracy.
  • Implements a sustain pedal by either: using head motion/pose or external input devices.
  • Outputs MIDI with loopMIDI on Windows.

Current Challenges:

  • Quest Hand-Tracking: introduces noticeable latency in collisions, and not supported in PCVR, hence making it impossible to output MIDI.
